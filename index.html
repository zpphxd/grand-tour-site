The practical rail playbook

01 · Transfer

Seven minutes is a minimum

Zürich HB’s default planner interchange is seven minutes—not a universal recommendation. Add time for luggage, bikes, children, unfamiliar platforms or reduced mobility.

Station plan
02 · Bike

Reserve the machine too

Bike Day Pass CHF 15. Many IC services need a CHF 2 reservation in season; EC, ICE and RJX spaces require reservations year-round.

Bike rules
03 · Delay

Keep proof and receipts

Swiss compensation starts at 60 minutes (25%) and reaches 50% at 120 minutes. Speak to staff before arranging an expensive last-connection alternative.

Claims and rights
04 · Access

Check the actual train

Accessibility detail can change with rolling stock. Swiss assistance generally needs one hour; shuttle cases may require two.

01Borders

EES is live. ETIAS is not yet live.

The EU reports that its Entry/Exit System became fully operational on 10 April 2026. ETIAS is expected in the last quarter of 2026, but an exact start date has not been announced. Treat social posts and third-party countdowns as secondary.

Check the official EU status →
02Disruptions

Know the remedy before you reroute.

Air, rail, bus and ferry rights differ. Save receipts, ask the operator to record the disruption and check the official mode-specific guidance before accepting an expensive replacement.

EU passenger rights →
03Money

Budget in ranges, pay in local currency.

Use ECB reference rates for planning, then expect the card network and issuer to settle differently. At a terminal or ATM, decline dynamic currency conversion unless you have compared its rate.

04Outdoors

The route is not the condition.

Mapped trails can be closed, snow-covered or unsafe. Check the local authority, refuge or lift operator, then download the route and bailout options offline. Weather-fit is guidance, never clearance.
